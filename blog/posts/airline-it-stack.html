Airline IT Stack: Why It’s Stuck (and How It Actually Works)

A detailed technical breakdown of the airline IT stack—GDS, PSS, direct channels, and the economic forces that keep modernization stalled despite clear incentives to improve.

Platform Engineering Systems Architecture

TL;DR

The airline industry runs on a layered stack built around Global Distribution Systems (GDS) and Passenger Service Systems (PSS). Direct channels are modern, but the shared backbone remains rigid, making it difficult to sell modern products and forcing the industry into a “reverse tragedy of the commons” where no single airline wants to pay the cost of interoperability upgrades alone.

1) The Stack — Who Does What?

The airline IT stack can be understood as a set of layers with very different incentives:

  • Customer Channels: OTAs (Expedia, Booking), travel agents, airline websites/apps.
  • Distribution: GDS (Sabre, Amadeus, Travelport) for availability, pricing, ticketing.
  • Airline Core: PSS for inventory, fares, ticketing, and check-in.
  • Ops/Fulfillment: boarding, baggage, upgrades, and airport systems.

2) Why GDS Still Runs the World

GDS is the interoperability layer. It allows agents and OTAs to assemble itineraries across airlines using decades‑old command-line systems. The problem: it’s based on rigid data models and rules that don’t express modern product features (bundles, flexible seating, dynamic upgrades). This blocks innovation at the distribution layer.

The result: airlines can innovate on their own apps and websites, but they can’t consistently expose those features through the GDS.

3) Direct Channels vs. Interoperability

Direct channels are where airlines make the most money. They can do:

  • Upsells (bags, seats, early boarding)
  • Credit card promos
  • Dynamic offers

But direct channels fragment the ecosystem. Customers lose the seamless ability to compare and combine itineraries across carriers. 

Legacy Path (GDS)
Customer -> OTA/Agent -> GDS -> PSS -> Ticket

Modern Path (Direct)
Customer -> Airline App -> PSS -> Upsell Engine -> Ticket + Ancillaries

4) The Economic Stalemate (Reverse Tragedy of the Commons)

Each airline is disincentivized to modernize interoperability alone. If one carrier upgrades but partners do not, it pays the cost without receiving network effects. In equilibrium, everyone stays on “good enough” systems.

This creates a systemic modernization deadlock—even though all parties would benefit if they moved together.

5) Why It’s a Public Concern

Airlines function as essential infrastructure. The public depends on them for long‑haul travel and rural connectivity. But the incentives are shareholder‑focused, not public‑service‑focused. That gap is why regulatory pressure is often the only force capable of shifting the technical equilibrium.
